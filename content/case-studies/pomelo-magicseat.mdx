MagicSeat was Pomelo's founding product — a virtual waiting room system born from a 7-hour ER wait. By the time I was leading it, the product had real traction: 337 Walmart pharmacy locations across Canada and 26 Exer Urgent Care clinics in the US. But the P&L was negative, the infrastructure carried significant technical debt, and a free competitor was eroding the Canadian market from below. The question wasn't whether MagicSeat worked. It was whether it was a standalone business or a platform capability — and who it was actually for.

## What I Did

**Diagnosed the business model mismatch before touching the product.** Walmart Canada ($45 CAD/license) and Exer US ($300 USD/license) told two completely different stories about value and willingness to pay. The SMS cost structure was destroying the P&L at Canadian pricing. First move: optimize automation and SMS cost model before any new feature investment. Efficiency before scope.

**Framed the strategic fork explicitly rather than letting it drift.** MagicSeat had two viable futures — standalone SaaS or embedded capability within a larger platform. Built the SWOT and business case to force that decision with stakeholders. The TELUS Health context made the acquisition thesis legible.

**Used the pandemic as a product accelerator, not a distraction.** Extended the existing MagicSeat architecture into the vaccine deployment workflow — controlled patient flow, geolocation queue management, predictive throughput — without fragmenting the core product.

**Held the line on configurable-not-custom architecture under enterprise pressure.** Both Walmart and Exer deployments carried pressure to customize. Maintained clinic-specific settings, not clinic-specific code — keeping deployment repeatable and the codebase maintainable at 300+ locations.

## Outcome

~$265K combined net ARR across Walmart Canada and Exer US at acquisition-stage evaluation. Product positioned as a plug-and-play SaaS capability complementary to EMR/EHR portfolios — the framing that made it legible to TELUS Health as a strategic fit, not a standalone bet.

## Reflection

The virtual waiting room's core value is predicting wait times accurately. With modern AI, I'd replace static SMS queue updates with a dynamic prediction engine trained on real appointment duration data per clinic type, provider, and time-of-day — dramatically improving accuracy and reducing patient anxiety. Post-vaccine adverse reaction monitoring is another unlock: an AI-driven symptom triage conversation via SMS rather than a static form would improve capture rates and surface concerning cases faster.
