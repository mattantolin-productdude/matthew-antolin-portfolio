## Problem

Pharma manufacturers run AI agents on their DTC and HCP brand websites — answering patient and prescriber questions about dosing, coverage, side effects, and access programs. These agents handle thousands of inquiries per month. The brand and commercial teams managing them had no efficient way to answer the questions that mattered most: Is our agent performing well? What are patients and HCPs actually asking? Which inquiries are going unresolved? Are users reaching high-value actions like finding a prescriber or requesting a sample?

The data existed. But surfacing it required pulling raw conversation logs, running analyst queries, and assembling monthly PPTX decks by hand — a cycle that was slow, expensive, and disconnected from the pace at which brand teams needed to act.

## Discovery

I ran discovery with pharma brand managers, commercial analytics leads, and medical affairs stakeholders across multiple manufacturer accounts. The core tension was consistent: the people who most needed performance visibility — brand managers deciding which agent responses to update, which topics to escalate — were the least equipped to query raw data themselves. Analysts were the bottleneck, and monthly cadences meant brand teams were always acting on stale information.

A secondary finding shaped the product architecture: brand teams didn't just want dashboards. They wanted to ask follow-up questions. "What percentage of those unresolved inquiries came from HCPs versus patients?" "Show me the top five external links clicked in August." Static dashboards couldn't support that. Conversational access could.

## Approach

**Conversational analytics interface.** Built the Piper Data Analyst agent — a natural-language interface scoped per drug brand and agent type (DTC, HCP). Users start from curated conversation prompts that represent the most common brand team questions, or ask their own. Piper queries the underlying engagement data and returns structured analysis: coverage rates, topic distributions, resolution rates, desired outcome click-throughs.

**Automated report generation.** Built an automated PPTX report pipeline that runs monthly per brand/agent pairing and makes reports available for download directly in the Piper UI. This eliminated the analyst reporting cycle for standard monthly reviews and shifted analyst time toward interpretation and action rather than assembly.

**Per-brand, per-agent architecture.** Structured the product around the natural hierarchy of the pharma market: manufacturer → brand → agent type (DTC vs. HCP). Each brand team sees only their own agent data. Navigation and agent context are scoped accordingly, with separate agent instances and report streams for DTC and HCP channels.

**High-Value Action (HVA) CTA serving.** Developed a PRD for serving contextually relevant CTAs to users of the brand's website agents based on conversation intent — surfacing "Find a Specialist" or "Request a Sample" links at the right moment in the conversation flow rather than as static sidebar elements. This connected Piper's analytics insights directly back to agent optimization.

**AI model evaluation framework.** As Piper's underlying models evolved, I needed a structured way to assess whether a new model version was better or worse before shipping to clients. Built a multi-step agent version comparator covering prompt input collection, topic clustering, difference and gap analysis, QA test-result evaluation, and impact scoring — giving the team a repeatable, documented process for model change decisions rather than ad hoc vibes-based comparisons.

## Outcome

Specific client metrics are confidential. Directionally: brand teams moved from monthly analyst-mediated reporting to on-demand self-service analytics. The automated report pipeline made standard monthly reviews zero-analyst-effort. The Data Analyst agent saw repeat usage from brand managers who had previously never directly accessed engagement data. The HVA CTA work opened a new product surface that gave Swoop a more direct line to client campaign optimization decisions.

## Reflection

The hardest design problem wasn't the analytics itself — it was the conversation starter UX. Brand managers are not data analysts, and a blank chat interface is intimidating in a way that a dashboard isn't. Getting the starter prompts right required several rounds of iteration with real users: too abstract and they didn't map to actual decisions; too specific and they felt like someone else's questions. The version that worked best led with outcomes ("Is my agent covering the questions patients actually have?") rather than metrics ("Show me coverage rate"). It was a small UX decision with outsized impact on adoption, and I wouldn't have gotten there without treating the starter prompt set as a first-class product artifact rather than a launch afterthought.
